2-Day Istanbul Travel Guide

Why go
Istanbul blends Byzantine, Ottoman, and modern Turkish culture into an easy-to-explore city split between Europe and Asia. With two full days, you can cover the blockbuster sights, sample classic flavors, and get out on the Bosphorus.

Day 1: Old City highlights and the Bosphorus

Morning: Sultanahmet essentials
- Hagia Sophia (Ayasofya): Arrive at opening to avoid queues. It is an active mosque; visitor access, policies, and any ticketing rules can change, so check the latest guidance before you go. Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered; women should bring a headscarf), remove shoes when required, and avoid visits during prayer times.
- Blue Mosque (Sultanahmet Camii): A few minutes’ walk from Hagia Sophia. It is also an active mosque with free entry; closes to tourists during prayers and around Friday noon prayers. Dress modestly; headscarves and shoe bags are typically provided.

Optional if time allows before lunch:
- Hippodrome (Sultanahmet Square) with the Obelisk of Theodosius and the Serpent Column.
- Basilica Cistern for atmospheric Byzantine architecture (paid entry, lines can be long).

Lunch: Classic kebab
- What to try: Adana (spicy minced lamb), Urfa (milder), İskender (slices of döner over pide with tomato-butter sauce and yogurt), cağ kebabı (horizontal spit), and dürüm (wraps).
- Where to look: Sirkeci, Sultanahmet side streets, or cross the Galata Bridge toward Karaköy for more options. If you want names, locals often recommend places like Hamdi Restaurant (near Eminönü) for views and kebabs, Şehzade Erzurum Cağ Kebap (Sirkeci) for cağ kebabı, or Kebapçı İskender (various branches) for İskender.

Afternoon: Grand Bazaar and Spice Market
- Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı): One of the world’s oldest covered markets. Browse carpets, ceramics, lamps, leather, jewelry, and textiles. See bargaining tips below.
- Spice Bazaar (Mısır Çarşısı) in Eminönü: Great for spices, teas, dried fruits, and Turkish delight. More food-focused than the Grand Bazaar.

Late afternoon to sunset: Bosphorus ferry cruise
- Public ferries: Affordable and scenic. From Eminönü, take a commuter ferry to Üsküdar or Kadıköy for a short crossing with skyline views.
- Bosphorus tours: The official Şehir Hatları “Short Bosphorus Tour” is a budget-friendly 1.5–2 hour loop that goes under the bridges and back. Sunset departures are especially beautiful.
- What to bring: A light jacket (it can be breezy on deck), camera, small bills for tea or simit on board.

Evening: Dinner and baklava
- Dinner: Try another kebab style you missed earlier, or opt for meze and seafood around Karaköy or the Galata Bridge.
- Baklava: Iconic spots include Karaköy Güllüoğlu (Karaköy), Hafız Mustafa (multiple branches), or Köşkeroğlu (Sirkeci/Karaköy). Order a mix: fıstıklı (pistachio), şöbiyet (with semolina cream), and havuç dilimi (a large pistachio slice). Pair with Turkish tea or coffee.

Day 2: Turkish breakfast, Asian side, and Istiklal nightlife

Morning: Turkish breakfast culture (kahvaltı)
- What’s on the table: Fresh bread, simit (sesame ring), cheeses, olives, tomatoes and cucumbers, jams, honey with kaymak (clotted cream), eggs (menemen—eggs scrambled with tomatoes and peppers—or sucuklu yumurta—eggs with spicy sausage), börek (savory pastry), and endless tea.
- Where to go: Beşiktaş’s “breakfast street” (Kahvaltıcılar Sokağı) on the European side; Karaköy cafes; or head to the Asian side for scenic options in Çengelköy or Kuzguncuk.
- Tip: Go hungry and take your time; breakfast is a social ritual.

Late morning to afternoon: Asian side wander
- Ferry to Kadıköy: Stroll the market streets, sample meze shops, coffee bars, and dessert spots. Walk to Moda for seaside views and a relaxed vibe.
- Alternatively, Üsküdar: Coastal promenade with views back to the Old City, Maiden’s Tower, and historic mosques.
- Snack ideas: Freshly squeezed pomegranate juice, midye dolma (stuffed mussels—choose reputable vendors), lahmacun (thin flatbread with minced meat), or a quick dürüm.

Return to Europe: Galata and Istiklal Avenue
- Galata: Walk up or down between Karaköy and Galata Tower. Side streets have boutiques and cafes.
- Istiklal Avenue (Beyoğlu): Pedestrian artery from Galata/Tünel to Taksim Square. Check out passages like Çiçek Pasajı and the nostalgic red tram.

Nightlife on and around Istiklal
- Meyhane experience: Traditional taverns serving meze and seafood, often with rakı (anise spirit). Neighborhoods include Asmalımescit and Nevizade (touristy but lively); you’ll also find excellent meyhanes tucked into side streets of Beyoğlu and Cihangir.
- Rooftops and bars: Beyoğlu and Karaköy have many rooftop terraces with Golden Horn or Bosphorus views. Look for live music venues and jazz bars around Galata and Çukurcuma.
- Tips: Check menus and prices before sitting; avoid being pressured inside by touts; stick to well-reviewed places.

Practical tips

Bargaining in the Grand Bazaar and Spice Market
- Where to bargain: Independent stalls for carpets, lamps, ceramics, leather, textiles, antiques. Fixed-price shops and food stalls sometimes negotiate less.
- How to start: Ask for the price, then politely counter 30–50% lower depending on the item and shop. Expect to meet somewhere in the middle.
- Do your homework: Learn basic quality markers (e.g., hand-knotted vs. machine-made carpets; real leather vs. faux). Ask about materials, origin, and care.
- Be polite and patient: Smile, chat, and accept tea if you like, but don’t feel obligated to buy. Walking away is an effective tactic if the price isn’t right.
- Receipts and shipping: If shipping a carpet or fragile goods, get proper paperwork and tracking. Understand customs rules; exporting genuine antiques may be restricted.
- Payment: Cash can yield better prices. If paying by card, ask about fees and confirm the final amount before authorizing.

Local transit and getting around
- Istanbulkart: The reusable transit card for metro, tram, buses, Marmaray, funiculars, and most ferries. Buy and top up at major stations and kiosks; one card can be tapped multiple times for a group.
- Contactless cards: Many gates accept contactless bank cards, but coverage can vary. Istanbulkart remains the most reliable for seamless transfers and discounts.
- Key lines for visitors:
  - T1 Tram: Runs through Sultanahmet (Old City) to Eminönü/Karaköy and up to Kabataş.
  - M2 Metro: Connects Taksim and Şişhane (near Galata) to other parts of the city.
  - Marmaray: An undersea rail line linking Europe and Asia quickly.
  - Ferries: Frequent, scenic links between Eminönü/Karaköy/Beşiktaş and Üsküdar/Kadıköy.
- Airports:
  - Istanbul Airport (IST): Havaist buses run to central hubs; there is also a metro link. Taxis and ride-hailing are available.
  - Sabiha Gökçen (SAW): Buses to Kadıköy/Taksim and other hubs; taxis and ride-hailing available.
- Taxis and ride-hailing: Use licensed yellow taxis. Ensure the meter is on; avoid flat fares. Apps like BiTaksi and Uber (which dispatch licensed taxis) improve reliability and transparency.
- Crowds and timing: The T1 tram and Sultanahmet get crowded midday; go early. Friday noon prayers affect mosque access. Allow extra time for peak-hour travel.

Respect and etiquette at mosques
- Dress modestly; women should bring a headscarf.
- Remove shoes where required; use provided bags.
- Stay quiet, avoid flash, and do not enter prayer areas during services.
- Check current visiting hours and any special rules for Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque.

Food and drink notes
- Turkish coffee is strong and unfiltered; let the grounds settle. Tea (çay) fuels daily life—accepting a small glass is part of the culture.
- Street food is excellent; choose busy vendors with high turnover.
- For dessert beyond baklava, try künefe (cheese pastry in syrup), sütlaç (rice pudding), or fıstıklı katmer (pistachio pastry).

Money, safety, and connectivity
- Currency: Turkish lira. Cards are widely accepted in restaurants and shops; carry some cash for markets and small purchases.
- ATMs: Use bank-owned ATMs and decline dynamic currency conversion to avoid poor rates.
- Safety: Istanbul is generally safe in tourist areas. Beware of pickpockets in crowded trams/markets and classic “bar scams.” If a stranger insists on taking you to a bar or tailor unprompted, politely decline.
- SIM and data: Airport and city kiosks sell tourist SIMs; many hotels and cafes have Wi‑Fi. Offline maps help in the Grand Bazaar’s maze.

Seasonal and planning tips
- Best light: Early mornings and sunset along the Bosphorus and around mosques.
- Lines: Buy timed tickets online where offered (e.g., Basilica Cistern) and arrive at opening for major sights.
- Weather: Evenings on the water can be cool; bring a layer year-round.
- Holidays: During Ramadan and public holidays, opening hours and crowds may differ.

Sample 2-day flow at a glance
- Day 1: Hagia Sophia and Blue Mosque → lunch kebab → Grand Bazaar and Spice Bazaar → sunset Bosphorus ferry → dinner and baklava.
- Day 2: Long Turkish breakfast → ferry to Kadıköy or Üsküdar and explore → Galata and Istiklal Avenue → meyhane or rooftop nightlife.

Enjoy your trip, and leave room in your schedule—and your appetite—for serendipity.
