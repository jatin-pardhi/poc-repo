2-Day Hanoi Travel Guide

Overview
- Best for first-time visitors who want Hanoi’s history, food, and a quick taste of Ha Long Bay.
- Includes: Old Quarter walking tour, Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum, Hoan Kiem Lake, a street food crawl (pho, banh mi, egg coffee), water puppet show, and a Ha Long Bay day trip.
- Getting around: Walk and use Grab (ride-hailing) or reputable taxis. The Old Quarter is compact but crowded.

Day 1: Hanoi City Highlights

Morning: Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum and Ba Dinh Area
- Aim to arrive by 7:15–7:30 am to beat queues.
- Opening pattern: Typically mornings only; often closed Mondays and Fridays; may close for maintenance Sep–Nov. Always check current hours before you go.
- Dress code and decorum: Shoulders and knees covered; no hats inside; no photography in the viewing hall; move in a single line.
- What to see nearby:
  - Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum at Ba Dinh Square.
  - One Pillar Pagoda (small but iconic).
  - Presidential Palace grounds (view from outside), Ho Chi Minh’s Stilt House, and the Ho Chi Minh Museum if you enjoy modern history.
- Practical tips: Minimal items for faster security; you may need to store bags/cameras.

Late Morning to Early Afternoon: Old Quarter Walking Tour to Hoan Kiem Lake
- Start at O Quan Chuong Gate (Old City Gate). Then a loop through craft streets and landmarks:
  - Hang Chieu and Hang Ma (paper goods and festive decorations).
  - Hang Bac (silversmiths) and Bach Ma Temple.
  - Heritage House at 87 Ma May (a restored traditional merchant home).
  - Ta Hien Street (beer street; lively at night).
  - St. Joseph’s Cathedral (French colonial-era; nice photo stop).
  - Hang Gai (silk, lacquerware) leading toward Hoan Kiem Lake.
- Hoan Kiem Lake circuit:
  - Ngoc Son Temple and The Huc Bridge for views and local history.
  - Turtle Tower (Thap Rua) is a photogenic centerpiece viewed from the shore.
- Optional note: “Train Street” access is often restricted for safety; only visit legally via cafes with permission and follow local guidance.

Street Food Crawl (Part 1: Lunch)
- Pho (beef or chicken): Old Quarter shops often run out after lunch; expect shared tables and quick service.
- Banh mi: Grab a baguette sandwich to-go if you want to keep moving.
- Bun cha (grilled pork with herbs and noodles): A Hanoi specialty, great for lunch.
- Hygiene tips:
  - Join places busy with locals and a fast turnover.
  - Watch food cooked to order; avoid lukewarm broths.
  - Carry tissues and hand sanitizer.

Afternoon: Coffee Break and Culture
- Egg coffee: A Hanoi invention, rich and dessert-like. Historic cafes are tucked in Old Quarter alleys and near the lake.
- Museum options if you have energy:
  - Vietnam Women’s Museum (well-curated and near the lake).
  - Vietnam National Museum of History (east of the lake).

Evening: Water Puppet Show and Night Bites
- Thang Long Water Puppet Theatre, near Hoan Kiem Lake.
  - Multiple nightly shows; popular times sell out. Book tickets earlier the same day or online.
  - Family friendly; narration is minimal, music and puppetry tell the story.
- Dinner and drinks:
  - Try cha ca (turmeric-dill fish cooked at the table) or more street eats (bun cha, banh cuon rice rolls).
  - Ta Hien beer street for a lively scene; keep personal items secure and drink responsibly.
- Night stroll: Return to Hoan Kiem Lake; weekends often feature walking streets and performances.

Day 2: Ha Long Bay Day Trip

Overview
- Expect a full day, roughly 7:30 am to 7:30–8:30 pm door-to-door.
- Choose an expressway transfer and a reputable operator. Small-group or premium day cruises are more relaxed.

Typical Day Trip Flow
- 7:30–8:00 am: Pickup in Hanoi Old Quarter.
- 2–2.5 hours: Drive via expressway to Tuan Chau or Ha Long International Cruise Port.
- 4–6 hour bay cruise:
  - Limestone karsts scenery.
  - Cave visit (e.g., Sung Sot/Surprise Cave) with stairs and uneven surfaces.
  - Kayaking or bamboo boat through grottoes (e.g., Luon Cave or Ba Hang).
  - Titov Island stop for a viewpoint hike or beach time (on some itineraries).
  - Onboard lunch (seafood-forward; request vegetarian or dietary needs in advance).
- 2–2.5 hours: Return to Hanoi in the late afternoon/evening.

Booking and Practical Tips
- Pickups: Confirm Old Quarter pickup, bus size, and whether it’s a direct expressway route.
- Safety: Boats should provide life jackets; ask where they are stored. Wear during kayaking.
- Seasickness: The bay is usually calm, but bring motion-sickness tablets if sensitive.
- Pack: Passport (sometimes checked), light jacket, sunscreen, hat, water bottle, power bank, small cash, towel if swimming, sandals that can get wet.
- Weather: Winter can be misty and cool; summer is hot and can bring sudden storms. Trips can be canceled for safety.
- Responsible travel: Avoid single-use plastic; don’t litter; respect cave rules.

Alternative in case of bad weather
- If Ha Long is canceled, consider a Ninh Binh day trip (Trang An boat ride, Hoa Lu temples, or Mua Cave viewpoint). Similar timeline.

Must-Try Food Checklist (Hanoi Focus)
- Pho bo or pho ga: Breakfast or lunch staple.
- Bun cha: Grilled pork with herbs and dipping sauce.
- Banh mi: Quick, customizable, ideal for a walking day.
- Egg coffee: Signature Hanoi treat.
- Cha ca: Turmeric-dill fish specialty for dinner.
- Bia hoi: Fresh draft beer; light and social.

Motorbike Traffic Survival Tips
- Crossing the street:
  - Pick a steady line and pace; don’t dart or stop abruptly.
  - Make eye contact with approaching riders; let them flow around you.
  - Use pedestrian crossings when available, but don’t assume cars will stop.
- On sidewalks: Expect occasional motorbikes and vendors; stay alert at curb cuts and alley mouths.
- Riding pillion or renting:
  - If inexperienced, use GrabBike rather than renting yourself. Always wear a helmet.
  - Hold on firmly, keep knees in, avoid loose bags or dangling straps.
- Bags and phones:
  - Keep valuables zipped and close to your body; avoid using phones at the curb.
- Rain and night:
  - Roads get slick quickly; drivers may have reduced visibility. Use ponchos; avoid last-minute dashes across roads.
- Horns and noise:
  - Honking is often a “here I am” signal, not aggression. Stay calm and predictable.

Practicalities
- Money: Vietnamese Dong (VND). Cash is common for small vendors; ATMs are widespread. Have small bills for street food.
- Connectivity: Local SIM or eSIM with data makes Grab and maps easy.
- Scams and touts: Politely decline cyclo or unsolicited tour offers if not interested; confirm prices before boarding any ride.
- Dress and respect: Temples and the Mausoleum expect modest clothing. Remove hats inside sacred spaces.
- Hydration and heat: It gets humid; carry water and pace yourself, especially mid-day in summer.
