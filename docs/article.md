2-Day Marrakech Travel Guide

Why go
- Marrakech blends centuries-old medina life with lush gardens, ornate palaces, soulful cuisine, and easy access to the Atlas Mountains. Two days is enough to catch the essentials, soak in the atmosphere, and step into Berber mountain villages just beyond the city.

Where to stay: traditional riads
- What they are: Restored townhouses hidden behind plain doors, built around a quiet courtyard with zellige tiles, carved plaster, and often a plunge pool and rooftop.
- Neighborhoods: 
  - Near Jemaa el-Fnaa for heart-of-the-action energy and short walks to souks.
  - Mouassine/Dar el Bacha for chic, calmer lanes.
  - Kasbah for proximity to palaces and tombs.
- Tips:
  - Ask your riad to arrange airport pickup and a porter for luggage; many alleys aren’t car accessible.
  - Save the pin for your riad’s closest gate; note the name of your nearest bab (city gate).
  - Expect calls to prayer and ambient medina noise; bring earplugs if you’re a light sleeper.

Day 1: Gardens, palaces, souks, and the square

Morning
- Majorelle Garden and Yves Saint Laurent Museum (8:30–10:30)
  - Reserve timed tickets in advance for the earliest slot to avoid crowds and midday heat.
  - Taxis drop at the entrance on Rue Yves Saint Laurent; carry small change.
  - Highlights: cobalt-blue villa, cactus collections, bamboo grove, Berber Museum (small but excellent).
- Coffee break nearby
  - Grab a fresh orange juice or mint tea before heading to the medina.

Late morning
- Bahia Palace (11:00–12:30)
  - A 19th-century gem with painted cedar ceilings, intricate tilework, and serene courtyards.
  - Go earlier for fewer tour groups; bring cash for tickets.
  - Combine with a short stroll past the Mellah (old Jewish quarter) if time allows.

Lunch
- Rooftop near the medina
  - Try chicken tagine with preserved lemon and olives or lamb tagine with prunes and almonds.
  - Order freshly baked khobz (bread), zaalouk (smoky eggplant), and finish with mint tea.
  - Note: Moroccan mint tea is sweet by default; say “bila sukkar” for less sugar.

Afternoon
- Souks of Marrakech (14:00–17:00)
  - Start at Souk Semmarine and branch into specialty lanes: dyers’ souk (Sebbaghine), metalworkers (Haddadine), leather (Cherratine), and woodwork.
  - Navigation:
    - Expect scooters and carts; keep right and step aside when you hear horns.
    - Streets that “look closed” often aren’t; ignore “It’s closed” or unsolicited guides offering help.
  - Haggling tips:
    - Browse first to gauge prices; don’t start bargaining unless you’re ready to buy.
    - A friendly, patient tone works best. Begin at 30–40% of the first price and aim to settle around 50–60%.
    - Know your walk-away price. If you leave politely, you may be called back with a better offer.
    - Pay in dirhams; cards incur fees and are less accepted in small shops.
    - Check quality: stitching on leather, weight and feel of brass, knot density on rugs, and food-grade certification for ceramics if needed.
    - Fixed-price cooperatives exist if you prefer no haggling, but prices are higher.

Sunset and evening
- Jemaa el-Fnaa (17:30–20:00)
  - Arrive before sunset to watch the square transform: orange juice stalls by day, storytellers, Gnawa musicians, and food tents by night.
  - For a view, take a seat on a rooftop terrace (Café de France or Le Grand Balcon) and listen for the Koutoubia call to prayer echoing at dusk.
  - Street smarts:
    - Always tip performers and storytellers if you watch or take photos.
    - Politely decline henna or photo ops with animals; avoid handlers with chained monkeys or snakes.
