A Trip to Japan: A practical 2-day plan with options for Tokyo or Kyoto

If you’ve only got two days in Japan, the key is focus. Pick one city, travel light, and book a few highlights in advance. This guide gives you two complete, time-efficient itineraries: one for Tokyo and one for Kyoto. Choose the city you’re flying into or the vibe you want (ultramodern energy vs. historic charm), plus practical tips to make every minute count.

Pre-trip essentials (do these before you go)
- Choose your base city: If you land at Haneda/Narita, pick Tokyo. If you land at Kansai (KIX), pick Kyoto. Avoid long intercity travel on a 2-day trip.
- Book a hotel near a major station: Tokyo (Shinjuku, Shibuya, Tokyo Station, Ginza), Kyoto (near Kyoto Station, Gion/Shijo area, Kawaramachi).
- Reserve popular tickets: Tokyo options include Shibuya Sky, teamLab Borderless or Planets, and some restaurants. Kyoto options include tea ceremonies, temple night illuminations (seasonal), or kaiseki.
- Connectivity: Get an eSIM or pocket Wi‑Fi.
- Transit: No JR Pass needed for 2 days. Use an IC card (Suica/PASMO/ICOCA) on phones or a physical card. If you can’t get Suica on your phone due to region issues, buy a PASMO Passport at the airport or major stations.
- Cash and cards: Japan is largely card-friendly in cities, but keep 5,000–10,000 yen cash for small shops, shrines, and lockers.
- Pack smart: Comfortable shoes, compact umbrella, layers. Bring a universal adapter if your plugs aren’t Type A. Japan is 100V; most modern electronics are fine.

Getting around efficiently
- Maps: Google Maps works very well for transit; HyperDia and Navitime are solid backups.
- IC card tips: Tap in/out for trains and buses; deposit may apply for physical cards; some tourist versions are non-refundable but convenient.
- Luggage: Use coin lockers at major stations or ask hotels to store bags before check-in/after check-out. For longer stays, Japan’s luggage forwarding (takkyubin) is amazing, but for 2 days you’ll likely just use lockers.
- Etiquette on trains: Keep your voice low, set phones to silent, avoid eating on local trains (Shinkansen is fine).

Etiquette quick hits
- Shoes off when entering homes, some traditional lodgings, fitting rooms, and certain restaurants; look for slippers.
- No tipping. A sincere thank you (arigatou gozaimasu) is perfect.
- Trash bins are rare; carry a small bag for your trash.
- Escalators: Stand on the left in Tokyo area; in Osaka area, stand on the right. Kyoto typically behaves like Tokyo, but follow locals.

How to choose your 2-day plan
- Tokyo: Go for towering skylines, neon nights, pop culture, and world-class dining. Minimal historical sites compared to Kyoto, but lots of variety.
- Kyoto: Temples, gardens, old streets, teahouses, riverside strolls, and calmer nights. Compact and atmospheric.

Itinerary A: 2 days in Tokyo

Where to stay
- Best convenience: Shinjuku or Shibuya for direct access to multiple lines, nightlife, dining, and airport connections.
- Quieter and central: Ginza or Tokyo Station area.
- Cool neighborhoods: Daikanyama, Nakameguro, or Asakusa (more local vibe, still convenient).

Day 1: Old meets new
Morning
- Asakusa and Sensō-ji: Arrive by 8:00–9:00 to beat crowds. Explore the temple grounds, Nakamise shopping street, and side alleys for traditional snacks like ningyo-yaki and senbei.
- Skytree view (optional): A quick hop across the river to Tokyo Skytree for morning cityscapes. If you prefer an evening view later, skip this now.

Late morning to lunch
- Ueno or Akihabara:
  - Ueno Park: Stroll the park and, if time permits, pick one museum (Tokyo National Museum is outstanding). Stop for taiyaki or coffee.
  - Akihabara: Browse electronics, retro games, arcades, and anime shops. Good for souvenirs.
- Lunch: Ramen shop with ticket machine ordering (fast and tasty), or a conveyor-belt sushi spot. If you prefer something lighter, grab onigiri at a convenience store to save time.

Afternoon
- Meiji Jingu Shrine: Tranquil forested shrine near Harajuku. A peaceful counterpoint to the city bustle.
- Harajuku and Omotesandō: Takeshita Street for youth fashion and crepes; Omotesandō for architecture and cafés.
- Shibuya: Cross the famous Scramble Crossing. If you want a view, reserve Shibuya Sky in advance for late afternoon or sunset.

Evening
- Dinner: Izakaya in Shibuya or Ebisu. Try yakitori, tempura, sashimi, and seasonal small plates. Remember no tipping.
- Night strolls: Omoide Yokocho (Shinjuku) or Golden Gai for atmospheric alleys and tiny bars. If you prefer a calmer vibe, walk along Meguro River (especially pretty in sakura season).

Day 2: Waterfront, modern art, and stunning views (or a traditional twist)
Morning
- Breakfast: Tsukiji Outer Market for street-side tamagoyaki, grilled seafood, and fresh sushi. Arrive early to avoid crowds.
- Option A: teamLab Borderless (Azabudai Hills) or teamLab Planets (Toyosu). These immersive digital museums require advance tickets and time slots. Planets is barefoot and very unique.
- Option B: Odaiba waterfront walk for city views and shopping. TeamLab used to be here; now enjoy sights like the Rainbow Bridge view.

Lunch
- Roppongi or Ginza: Easy for quality dining. In Roppongi, you can pair lunch with Mori Art Museum or Tokyo City View if you love art and panoramas. In Ginza, opt for a sushi set lunch or katsu. Department store food halls (depachika) are fantastic quick options.

Afternoon
- Traditional neighborhood option: Yanaka Ginza for a nostalgic, low-rise area with street snacks and crafts. Combine with nearby Nezu Shrine if time allows.
- Spa/onsen experience: For an urban soak, try Spa LaQua (Tokyo Dome City). Follow onsen etiquette: rinse thoroughly first, no swimsuits, cover tattoos if required (policies vary).

Sunset and evening
- Views: Choose one, reserve if needed. Shibuya Sky, Tokyo City View in Roppongi, Tokyo Tower main deck, or Tokyo Skytree for the highest view.
- Dinner: Choose a theme—omakase sushi, tempura counter, or a contemporary izakaya. If reservations are tight, go early or choose casual chains like Torikizoku for yakitori.
- Late night: Walk through Shinjuku Kabukicho’s neon (stick to main streets), or wind down in Daikanyama’s cafés and bars.

Time-savers and notes for Tokyo
- Transit lines to know: JR Yamanote Line loops many major hubs. Metro systems are dense; IC card keeps it simple.
- Shopping: If you’re short on time, focus on a single area (Ginza for upscale, Harajuku/Omotesandō for fashion, Akihabara for electronics).
- Ghibli Museum requires tickets far in advance; skip if you haven’t booked.

Itinerary B: 2 days in Kyoto

Where to stay
- Near Kyoto Station for easy buses/trains and luggage storage, or near Gion/Shijo-Kawaramachi for atmospheric evenings and dining.
- Expect more buses than subways; buses can be crowded during peak seasons. Use IC cards for simplicity.

Day 1: Southern and eastern Kyoto highlights
Early morning
- Fushimi Inari Taisha: Arrive by sunrise or by 7:00 to enjoy the torii gates with fewer people. Walk partway up for great photos; the full loop can take 2–3 hours, but you can turn back whenever you like.

Late morning
- Kiyomizu-dera: Head to Higashiyama’s preserved streets. Approach Kiyomizu-dera via Sannenzaka and Ninenzaka. Explore the temple’s wooden stage and spring. Try matcha soft serve or yatsuhashi nearby.

Lunch
- Gion or Higashiyama: Light soba/udon, tofu dishes (Kyoto specialty), or tempura set. If you want a tea experience, book a short tea ceremony.

Afternoon
- Gion and Shirakawa: Stroll historic lanes and small shrines. Keep respectful—no intrusive photography of residents. If you see geiko/maiko, give them space.
- Nishiki Market: Narrow food market with local ingredients and snacks. Great for souvenirs like pickles, tea, and ceramics.

Evening
- Pontocho Alley: Atmospheric riverside dining lane. Reserve a spot with a view if possible. Alternatively, dine around Kawaramachi for a variety of izakaya and noodle shops.
- Night walk: Along the Kamo River or back through Gion’s softly lit streets.

Day 2: Arashiyama and northern temples
Early morning
- Arashiyama Bamboo Grove: Arrive at dawn for serene paths and the best photos. Visit Tenryu-ji temple and its gardens right nearby.

Late morning
- Okochi Sanso Villa: Beautiful villa gardens with city views. If you prefer something more active and don’t mind a short climb, the Iwatayama Monkey Park is an option, but it takes time and energy.
- Saga-Toriimoto: If you love old houses and quiet lanes, wander a bit away from the main drag.

Lunch
- Arashiyama area: River-view cafés or tempura/soba shops. Alternatively, grab a bento for a riverside picnic.

Afternoon choices (pick one cluster to avoid rushing)
- Northern temples route: Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion), then Ryoan-ji (famous rock garden), and if time allows, Ninna-ji. Transit can be bus-heavy; consider a taxi between Kinkaku-ji and Ryoan-ji to save time.
- Eastern philosopher’s stroll: Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion) and the Philosopher’s Path. Calm and beautiful, especially in spring and autumn. Combine with a nearby tea stop.

Evening
- Final dinner: Try kaiseki for a tasting of seasonal Kyoto cuisine (book ahead), or keep it casual with yakitori or okonomiyaki. For dessert, look for warabimochi or matcha parfaits.

Time-savers and notes for Kyoto
- Start early each day to avoid crowds at Fushimi Inari, Kiyomizu-dera, and Arashiyama.
- Buses are convenient but can be slow; use trains where possible or short taxi hops between clusters to save time.
- Many temples close by 5 pm; plan indoor attractions earlier.

If you’re tempted to do both cities
- The Shinkansen between Tokyo and Kyoto takes about 2 hours 15 minutes one-way, plus station transfers. For a 2-day trip, it’s not worth the time and cost. Choose one city and go deep.

Weather and seasonal planning
- Spring (late March–early April): Cherry blossoms attract big crowds; book everything early and start your days at sunrise.
- Summer (June–August): Hot and humid; carry water, sunscreen, and a small towel. Expect sudden showers in rainy season (June/July).
- Autumn (mid-November): Fall foliage is spectacular; crowds similar to cherry blossom season. Book ahead.
- Winter (Dec–Feb): Cold but clear skies; great for uncrowded photos and onsen time.

Costs and quick budgeting (per person, per day, rough)
- Transit: 1,000–2,000 yen depending on distance and frequency.
- Food: 3,000–8,000 yen depending on style (convenience store breakfasts and noodle lunches are affordable; sushi/kaiseki elevate costs).
- Attractions: 0–4,000 yen (temple fees are modest; observatories and special exhibits cost more).
- Views and premiums: Observatory tickets or special experiences can add 2,000–4,000 yen.

Food tips
- Breakfast is often light in Japan; cafés open later than convenience stores. Grab onigiri, fruit, and coffee from convenience stores to start early.
- Ramen ordering via ticket machines is efficient. Return trays neatly and wipe your area if cloths are provided.
- Vegetarian and vegan options exist but can be limited at traditional spots. Look up dedicated restaurants or modern cafés; ingredients like dashi may contain fish.
- No tipping—service charge may be included at higher-end restaurants.

Language and basics
- Handy phrases: Sumimasen (excuse me), Arigatou gozaimasu (thank you), Onegaishimasu (please), Eigo wa hanasemasu ka? (Do you speak English?), Kono chizu de oshiete kudasai (Please show me on this map).
- Emergency: 110 for police, 119 for ambulance/fire.
- Restrooms: Abundant in stations, department stores, and convenience stores; often very clean.

Sample daily time plan (Tokyo example)
- 07:30 Breakfast on the go
- 08:30 Sensō-ji and Asakusa
- 10:30 Ueno Park or Akihabara
- 12:30 Lunch
- 14:00 Meiji Jingu, Harajuku
- 16:00 Shibuya crossing and café break
- 17:30 Shibuya Sky sunset (reserved)
- 19:30 Dinner in Shibuya/Ebisu
- 21:00 Night stroll or bar

Sample daily time plan (Kyoto example)
- 06:30 Fushimi Inari
- 09:00 Kiyomizu-dera via Higashiyama lanes
- 12:00 Lunch near Gion
- 13:30 Gion/Shirakawa stroll; Nishiki Market
- 17:00 Rest and refresh at hotel
- 18:30 Dinner in Pontocho
- 20:30 Kamo River evening walk

Departure day strategy
- Keep your last morning close to your station or airport line. If flying from Haneda/Narita, factor in 1–2 hours transit plus early arrival at the airport. From Kyoto to KIX, allow at least 1.5–2 hours via Limited Express Haruka or similar.
- Use coin lockers to free your hands for final strolls and souvenir shopping.

The takeaway
- Two days in Japan can be magical if you focus. Tokyo delivers high-energy contrasts and sky-high views; Kyoto gives you quiet lanes, vibrant shrines, and garden calm. Start early, pre-book a couple of highlights, use an IC card, and eat well but simply. With this plan, you’ll leave already plotting your return.
